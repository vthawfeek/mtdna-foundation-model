In your cells, there are two instruction manuals.

One is in the nucleus — 3 billion letters long, split across 23 chromosomes.

The other lives in tiny power plants called mitochondria. It's circular, only 16,569 letters, and passed down entirely through your mother's line.

Mutations in that second manual cause 350+ inherited diseases: MELAS, Leigh syndrome, Leber's hereditary optic neuropathy.

I spent 4 weeks building an AI to read it. Here's what I found. 🧵
---
The challenge: every AI trained to read DNA was trained on the 3-billion-letter version.

The mitochondrial genome is different in two important ways that break existing models:

1. It's circular — the end connects back to the start
2. It can carry two different versions of the same gene at once (called heteroplasmy)

Neither DNABERT2, HyenaDNA, nor Nucleotide Transformer was designed for this.

So I built one that was.
---
The first design decision: how do you encode a circle?

Standard transformer position encodings treat position 1 and position 16,569 as far apart. But in a circular genome, they're neighbours — one base apart.

Fix: replace the linear position counter with a clock face. Each position gets an angle between 0° and 360°. Position 0 and position 16,569 get the same angle.

The model now knows the genome wraps around.
---
Pre-training: I started with ~117,500 mitochondrial genomes from vertebrates — fish, mammals, birds, reptiles.

No labels. No annotations. Just: here is a sequence, predict the missing k-mers.

Then I continued pre-training on 34,974 human mitochondrial genomes from HmtDB (152,484 total training sequences).

After training, I ran a test: embed 100 human sequences, visualise with t-SNE. No haplogroup labels.

African, European, and East Asian lineages separated into distinct clusters.

The model learned human phylogeny from sequence patterns alone.
---
Three downstream tasks:

1. Haplogroup classification (26 groups): ~50% zero-shot k-NN vs 3.8% random
   Fine-tuned accuracy: 1.83% (2 CPU epochs, partial class collapse — not converged; zero-shot is the real signal)

2. Pathogenic variant prediction: architecture built (variant-token hidden state, LoRA r=4)
   Real evaluation: no labeled dataset available; AUROC unknown

3. Heteroplasmy regression: model architecture built; evaluation not performed
---
The result that surprised me most:

I fed in two genomes from ancient hominins — Neanderthal and Denisovan. Zero fine-tuning. Never in training data.

Both placed outside modern human variation, near the phylogenetic root, consistent with paleoanthropological consensus.

The model found what molecular anthropologists established from decades of fossil evidence — from k-mer patterns alone.
