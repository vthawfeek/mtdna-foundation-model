A pre-trained mtDNA model with no haplogroup labels during training produces embeddings where related haplogroups cluster together in 2-D. L-clade (African root), H/HV (European), and M-clade (East Asian) sequences separate visibly. The silhouette score confirms the separation is real in the full 256-d space, not just a t-SNE artefact.

This is Day 25 of building the first foundation model for mitochondrial DNA, a molecule whose mutations drive over 350 inherited diseases including MELAS, Leigh syndrome, and Leber hereditary optic neuropathy.

What the showcase notebook tests is not what the model was trained to do, but what it learned without being asked. Three results stand out:

Zero-shot k-NN accuracy: ~50% on haplogroup identification with no fine-tuning labels — this is the real headline from pre-training. The fine-tuned LoRA classifier reached 1.83% on the test set (below the 3.85% random baseline) after 2 CPU epochs; the class weighting helped but convergence requires more compute. The confusion errors concentrate between phylogenetically adjacent clades (L0/L1, H/HV), which tells you the embedding geometry is capturing evolutionary structure even when the classifier head is not yet calibrated.

Pathogenicity prediction: the architecture exists (variant-token hidden state, LoRA r=4) but no labeled evaluation dataset was prepared during this project. Real AUROC is unknown.

Ancient DNA: Neanderthal and Denisovan embeddings land outside modern human variation, near the phylogenetic root, consistent with paleoanthropological consensus — with no fine-tuning and no ancient sequences in training.
