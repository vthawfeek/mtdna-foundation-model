Pre-trained an mtDNA model on cross-species vertebrate genomes. No haplogroup labels. Then embedded 100 human sequences and ran t-SNE.

L-clade (African root), H/HV (European), M-clade (East Asian) separated into distinct clusters. Silhouette score > 0. Phylogeny from sequence patterns alone.
---
Confusion matrix for the fine-tuned 26-way haplogroup classifier:

Fine-tuned accuracy: 1.83% (test set, 73k windows). Random baseline: 3.8%. Two CPU epochs, partial class collapse to 3/26 classes — the LoRA adaptation did not converge at this compute budget.

Zero-shot k-NN with no labels: ~50%. That's the real signal from pre-training.

The confusion errors (L0↔L1, H↔HV) follow phylogenetic adjacency even in the partially collapsed model — the embedding geometry, not the classifier head, is doing the work.
---
Pathogenicity prediction:

Architecture trained (variant-token hidden state, LoRA r=4). No labeled evaluation dataset was prepared during this project, so AUROC is unknown. The model exists; the benchmark doesn't.

The architectural choice — hidden state at the variant token, not CLS — remains the right inductive bias for a local property like pathogenicity. Future work when labeled data is available.

Then I fed in Neanderthal and Denisovan mtDNA. Zero fine-tuning. Never in training.

Both appeared outside modern human diversity, near the phylogenetic root. Most similar to L-clade (root African). Neanderthal and Denisovan clustered near each other.

Consistent with what molecular anthropologists established from decades of fossil evidence.
---
Final test: embed each of the 37 mtDNA genes, no labels. Cluster.

Protein-coding genes, tRNA genes, and rRNA genes separated. The two rRNA genes (RNR1, RNR2) cluster together and apart from everything else. Functional categories from k-mer patterns alone.
