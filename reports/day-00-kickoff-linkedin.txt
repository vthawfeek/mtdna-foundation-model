I just spent 4 weeks building the first dedicated foundation model for mitochondrial DNA — from scratch, in public, documenting every day.

Here's what I built, what it learned, and what surprised me.

---

Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) is a 16,569-letter circular genome that every human inherits entirely from their mother. Mutations in it cause over 350 inherited diseases — including MELAS, Leigh syndrome, and Leber's hereditary optic neuropathy.

No sequence model had been designed specifically for it. Existing DNA foundation models (DNABERT2, HyenaDNA, Nucleotide Transformer) are trained on nuclear DNA, and they can't handle the two things that make mtDNA different: its circular topology and heteroplasmy (carrying two versions of the same gene at once).

So I built one that could.

---

What I built:

• Pre-trained a BERT-style encoder on 152,484 mitochondrial genomes (~117k cross-species vertebrates in Phase 1, then ~35k human from HmtDB in Phase 2)
• Novel circular positional encoding: replaces the linear position counter with a clock face so the model knows positions 1 and 16,569 are neighbours
• Heteroplasmy projection channel: encodes the continuous fraction of mutant vs. wild-type at each position
• Three fine-tuned tasks implemented: haplogroup classification (26-way), pathogenic variant prediction (binary), heteroplasmy level regression
• Live Gradio demo on HuggingFace Spaces — classify a sequence in your browser

---

Key results:

• Zero-shot haplogroup k-NN: ~50% accuracy with no fine-tuning labels (vs. 3.8% random) — the pre-training learned evolutionary structure from sequence patterns alone
• Fine-tuned haplogroup classifier: 1.83% test accuracy after 2 CPU epochs; partial class collapse — fine-tuning did not converge at this compute budget; zero-shot k-NN is the more honest signal
• Pathogenicity prediction: architecture and training code built; real evaluation not completed (no labeled variant dataset was prepared)
• Zero-shot ancient DNA: Neanderthal and Denisovan embeddings placed outside modern human variation, near the phylogenetic root — consistent with paleoanthropology, with no ancient sequences in training
