I built a foundation model for mtDNA on a laptop CPU over 25 days. Here's what I'd change about the architecture, training, and evaluation.

---
Building the pathogenicity model before the eval dataset was backwards. I had the architecture (LoRA r=4, binary head), the ClinVar download code, the training loop. What I didn't have: a labeled eval file. The right order is eval dataset first, then the model to beat it.
---
The GPU budget lesson in one number: 325 hours CPU vs 50 minutes A100 for the same 50-epoch LoRA fine-tuning run. A Colab Pro session costs about $20. I didn't spend it at the right point in the project and the fine-tuning story is incomplete as a result.
---
Scope lesson: I built three downstream task heads (haplogroup classification, pathogenic variant prediction, heteroplasmy regression). Evaluated end-to-end: haplogroup only, and only zero-shot. One task fully evaluated would have been a clearer result than three tasks partially evaluated.
---
Data lesson: HmtDB is approximately 60-70% European haplogroups. I knew this from Day 4 EDA. I didn't stratify the training sample until it became visible as partial class collapse at evaluation. Stratified sampling is a one-day fix that should have come before any model training.
---
Architecture lesson: the circular PE is mathematically correct. Position 0 and position 16,568 encode as adjacent, which is what the genome topology requires. But "correct" and "demonstrably better than sinusoidal PE" are different claims. I made the second claim without running the experiment.
---
Same for the het channel. Projecting a continuous heteroplasmy float into embedding space via nn.Linear(1, 256) is the right design. MELAS severity depends on whether m.3243A>G is at 40% or 80%. A sequence-only model can't tell. But I didn't run a controlled comparison showing it helps vs hurts training.
---
The masking rate question I'd also test: I used 15% (standard BERT). With a 4,096-token vocabulary covering only mtDNA, the reconstruction problem may be too easy. A 25-30% rate might force harder representations. One config change, one training run.
---
What I'd keep unconditionally: the circular PE math is the right description of the molecule. The het channel design is the right architecture for continuous variant data. The two-phase pre-training structure is correct. The execution gaps are all fixable without changing the architecture.
---
Update on the baseline lesson: DNABERT-2 was run for the bioRxiv preprint. DNABERT-2 zero-shot: 66.3%. mtDNA-FM: 37.9% (26-class, 95% CI 34.4–41.2%). The gap is clade-specific: African L haplogroups (F1 avg 0.692), West Eurasian haplogroups (F1 avg 0.096). On haplogroups C, F, E (diagnostic positions >3,000 nt) mtDNA-FM outperforms DNABERT-2.
