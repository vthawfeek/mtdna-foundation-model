My haplogroup classifier scored 1.83% on the test set. Random baseline is 3.85%. Below random.

Here's the diagnosis — and why the confusion matrix told a more useful story than the accuracy number.

The model has partial class collapse: only 3 of 26 haplogroup classes show F1 > 0.01. The other 23 classes are being predicted at near-zero rate. The model isn't guessing randomly — it's confidently predicting the same 3 haplogroups for everything.

Why? The 1,267 training genomes produce 82,355 windows after sliding-window tokenization. Larger genomes produce more windows — H haplogroup ends up as ~15.6% of all training windows; L5 is ~0.4%. Unweighted CrossEntropyLoss minimizes average loss by predicting the majority class. I added inverse-frequency class weights, which expanded collapse from 1 class to 3 — a partial fix, not a solution.

The deeper problem is compute. 2 epochs on CPU took 20 hours. Training loss after 2 epochs: 3.266. Random baseline: ln(26) = 3.258. The loss moved 0.008. The LoRA adaptation needs far more gradient updates to converge — GPU compute would give 100–200× throughput and make 50+ epoch runs feasible.

What's interesting: even at 1.83% accuracy with partial collapse, the errors are phylogenetically structured. When the model misclassifies, it predicts an adjacent haplogroup (L0↔L1, H↔HV) — never a cross-clade error like L3↔H. That's the pre-trained embedding geometry working, independent of the classifier head.

Zero-shot k-NN on the same embeddings: ~50% — no labels, no fine-tuning. That's what the pre-training actually learned about evolutionary structure.

The fine-tuned accuracy is a statement about CPU budget, not about what the model knows. Choosing the right metric for the right question matters.
